# Basic Overview of Historical Ciphers

Secrets have always played a crucial role in human history.  Leaders need to provide confidential information to their generals and merchants need to protect their trade advantages. The art of secret communication is called cryptography, and the tools used to achieve it are called ciphers.  In this lecture, some popular ciphers that appear more like Sunday newspaper puzzles than modern ciphers. However, these examples are the ancestors to modern ciphers and were considered hard to break when the only calculation tool was an abacus.

## Caesar Cipher

Since mathematics is at the heart of cryptography, plaintext messages are often represented as sequences of numbers.  For basic messages, we can disregard some grammar components, such as punctuation, and focus on the letters.

Suppose we only consider the 26 letters of the English alphabet.  We can represent each letter as a number from 0 to 25 and use the number 26 for spaces. For example, the message "HELLO WORLD" can be represented as the sequence of numbers:

$7\ 4\ 11\ 11\ 14\ 26\ 22\ 14\ 17\ 11\ 3$

Obviously, it would not take long for an adversary to guess the message if we just sent the numbers.

The Caesar Cipher adds a little bit of complexity by shifting the letters by a fixed amount.  For example, if we shift each letter by 3, then the message "HELLO    WORLD" would be represented as:

$10\ 7\ 14\ 14\ 17\ 26\ 25\ 17\ 20\ 14\ 6$

Perhaps the shift is not always 3, but it is some number between 1 and 25.  This cipher is called the Caesar Cipher because Julius Caesar used it to protect his messages.  It is also known as a shift cipher or a substitution cipher.

Mathematically, we simply assume the following standard letter to number mapping:
```A -> 0
B -> 1
C -> 2
D -> 3
...
Z -> 25
Space -> 26
```

Then, to encrypt a message, we can use the following formula:
```E(x) = (x + k) mod 27```
where `x` is the number representing the letter, `k` is the shift amount, and `E(x)` is the encrypted letter.

To decrypt the message, we can use the following formula:
```D(y) = (y - k) mod 27```
where `y` is the number representing the encrypted letter, `k` is the shift amount, and `D(y)` is the decrypted letter.

## Vignere Cipher

The Caesar Cipher is a simple substitution cipher, but it can be easily broken by trying all possible shifts.  To make it more secure, we can use a keyword to determine the shift for each letter.  This is called the Vignere Cipher.  For example, if we use the keyword "KEY", then the first letter of the message will be shifted by the letter 'K', the second letter will be shifted by the letter 'E', and the third letter will be shifted by the letter 'Y'.  Then, the fourth letter will be shifted by the letter 'K' again, and so on.  This way, the shift is not constant and depends on the position of the letter in the message.

Mathematically, we can represent the keyword as a sequence of numbers, where each letter is mapped to a number as before.  For example, the keyword "KEY" can be represented as:

```10 4 24```

Then, to encrypt a message, we can use the following formula:

```E(x_i) = (x_i + k_j) mod 27```

where `x_i` is the number representing the i-th letter of the message, `k_j` is the number representing the j-th letter of the keyword (where j = i mod length of keyword), and `E(x_i)` is the encrypted letter.

To decrypt the message, we can use the following formula:

```D(y_i) = (y_i - k_j) mod 27```

where `y_i` is the number representing the i-th letter of the encrypted message, `k_j` is the number representing the j-th letter of the keyword (where j = i mod length of keyword), and `D(y_i)` is the decrypted letter.


## Hill Cipher

The Vignere Cipher is more secure than the Caesar Cipher, but it can still be broken by analyzing the frequency of letters in the encrypted message.  To make it even more secure, we can use matrix multiplication and matrix inverses to encrypt and decrypt messages.

Recall that a matrix is a rectangular array of numbers and that only square matrices have inverses. The Hill Cipher uses a square matrix as the key to encrypt and decrypt messages.  For example, if we use an $n \times n$ matrix as the key, then we can encrypt a message by dividing it into blocks of size $n$ and multiplying each block by the key matrix.  To decrypt the message, we can multiply the encrypted blocks by the inverse of the key matrix.

For example, consider the following 3 x 3 key matrix:

$K = \begin{bmatrix}
2 & 3 & 1 \\
1 & 2 & 3 \\
3 & 1 & 2 \\
\end{bmatrix}
$

Now consider the message "HELLO WORLD".  We can represent it as a sequence of numbers as before:

$7\ 4\ 11\ 11\ 14\ 26\ 22\ 14\ 17\ 11\ 3$

We can divide the message into blocks of size 3:

$(7, 4, 11),\ (11, 14, 26),\ (22, 14, 17),\ (11, 3)$

In order for matrix multiplication to work, we need every block to have three numbers. We can simply add a padding character (e.g., space) to the last block to make it have three numbers:
$(7, 4, 11),\ (11, 14, 26),\ (22, 14, 17),\ (11, 3, 26)$

To encrypt the message, we can multiply each block by the key matrix:

$C_i = K \cdot M_i$

where $M_i$ is the i-th block of the message and $C_i$ is the i-th block of the encrypted message (cipher block).

$C_1 = K \cdot M_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 7 \\ 4 \\ 11 \end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix}2 & 3 & 1 \\
1 & 2 & 3 \\
3 & 1 & 2 \\
\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 7*2 + 4*3 + 11*1 \\ 7*1 + 4*2 + 11*3 \\ 7*3 + 4*1 + 11*2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 35 \\ 49 \\ 53 \end{bmatrix} \mod 27 = \begin{bmatrix} 8 \\ 22 \\ 26 \end{bmatrix}$

$C_2 = K \cdot M_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 11 \\ 14 \\ 26 \end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix}2 & 3 & 1 \\
1 & 2 & 3 \\
3 & 1 & 2 \\
\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 11*2 + 14*3 + 26*1 \\ 11*1 + 14*2 + 26*3 \\ 11*3 + 14*1 + 26*2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 97 \\ 133 \\ 117 \end{bmatrix} \mod 27 = \begin{bmatrix} 16 \\ 22 \\ 9 \end{bmatrix}$

$C_3 = K \cdot M_3 = \begin{bmatrix} 22 \\ 14 \\ 17 \end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix}2 & 3 & 1 \\
1 & 2 & 3 \\
3 & 1 & 2 \\
\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 22*2 + 14*3 + 17*1 \\ 22*1 + 14*2 + 17*3 \\ 22*3 + 14*1 + 17*2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 117 \\ 133 \\ 117 \end{bmatrix} \mod 27 = \begin{bmatrix} 9 \\ 22 \\ 9 \end{bmatrix}$

$C_4 = K \cdot M_4 = \begin{bmatrix} 11 \\ 3 \\ 26 \end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix}2 & 3 & 1 \\
1 & 2 & 3 \\
3 & 1 & 2 \\
\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 11*2 + 3*3 + 26*1 \\ 11*1 + 3*2 + 26*3 \\ 11*3 + 3*1 + 26*2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 73 \\ 89 \\ 85 \end{bmatrix} \mod 27 = \begin{bmatrix} 19 \\ 8 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix}$

The plaintext message blocks have been encrypted to the following cipher blocks:

$(8, 22, 26),\ (16, 22, 9),\ (9, 22, 9),\ (19, 8, 4)$

We then transmit these cipher blocks to the recipient.  We assume the recipient knows the key matrix and can compute its inverse. The inverse of the key matrix is:


$K^{-1} = \begin{bmatrix}
11 & 17 & 20 \\
20 & 11 & 17 \\
17 & 20 & 11 \\
\end{bmatrix}
$

To decrypt the message, the recipient can multiply each cipher block by the inverse of the key matrix:
$M_i = K^{-1} \cdot C_i$

For example, to decrypt the first cipher block:
$M_1 = K^{-1} \cdot C_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 8 \\ 22 \\ 26 \end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix}
11 & 17 & 20 \\
20 & 11 & 17 \\
17 & 20 & 11 \\
\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 8*11 + 22*17 + 26*20 \\ 8*20 + 22*11 + 26*17 \\ 8*17 + 22*20 + 26*11 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 7 \\ 4 \\ 11 \end{bmatrix} \mod 27 = \begin{bmatrix} 7 \\ 4 \\ 11 \end{bmatrix}$

This gives us the original message block (7, 4, 11), which corresponds to the letters "H", "E", and "L".  The recipient can repeat this process for the remaining cipher blocks to recover the entire plaintext message "HELLO WORLD".

$M_2 = K^{-1} \cdot C_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 16 \\ 22 \\ 9 \end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix}
11 & 17 & 20 \\
20 & 11 & 17 \\
17 & 20 & 11 \\
\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 11 \\ 14 \\ 26 \end{bmatrix}$
$M_3 = K^{-1} \cdot C_3 = \begin{bmatrix} 9 \\ 22 \\ 9 \end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix}
11 & 17 & 20 \\
20 & 11 & 17 \\
17 & 20 & 11 \\
\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 22 \\ 14 \\ 17 \end{bmatrix}$
$M_4 = K^{-1} \cdot C_4 = \begin{bmatrix} 19 \\ 8 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix}
11 & 17 & 20 \\
20 & 11 & 17 \\
17 & 20 & 11 \\
\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 11 \\ 3 \\ 26 \end{bmatrix}$


## Typical Attacks on Historical Ciphers

It is important to remember that simply making a cipher more complex does not necessarily make it significantly more secure.

For example, the Caesar Cipher can be easily broken by trying all possible shifts. An exhaustive brute force attack involving only 26 possible shifts can quickly reveal the original message. A malicious person would only need to decode a few blocks for a given attempt to determine it is not the correct shift because the resulting message would not make sense.

In general, some letters of the English language appear more frequently than others. For example, the letter 'E' is the most common letter in English text.  Therefore, if we analyze the frequency of letters in the encrypted message, we can make educated guesses about which letters correspond to which.  This is called a frequency analysis attack.

## Comparing Plaintext and Ciphertext

In some cases, malicious actors may have access to both the plaintext and the corresponding ciphertext.  This is called a known-plaintext attack.  By analyzing the relationship between the plaintext and the ciphertext, they can potentially recover the key used for encryption. All of the examples in this lecture are extremely vulnerable to known-plaintext attacks.

## Mechanical Cipher Machines

In the early 20th century, mechanical cipher machines were developed to automate the encryption and decryption process.  These machines used a combination of rotors, gears, and electrical circuits to perform complex substitutions and permutations on the plaintext.  One of the most famous mechanical cipher machines is the Enigma machine used by the Germans during World War II.  The Enigma machine was considered unbreakable at the time, but it was eventually cracked by Allied codebreakers, which played a significant role in the outcome of the war.

## Conclusion

Modern cryptographic techniques have evolved significantly from these historical ciphers.  While the Caesar Cipher, Vignere Cipher, and Hill Cipher were innovative for their time, they are now considered completely irrelevant for all but puzzles and games.

Modern cipher tools must be robust against the following:
- Brute force attacks
- Frequency analysis attacks
- Known-plaintext attacks

Basically, modern cryptographic tools must be secure even if an adversary is completely aware of the encryption and decryption algorithms being used.  The only thing that should be secret is the key. In general, this is the principle implemented in modern cryptographic systems.
